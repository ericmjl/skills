# Proof, closes, and ethical persuasion

*Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.*

Reference for building credibility when you are new, closing without sounding salesy, drawing the persuasion line, and deciding when to hire a copywriter.

---

## Proof elements when you have no testimonials yet

The real goal is **proof**, not testimonials. When a reader's brain asks "Why should I believe you?", proof is what answers it. You can sell before you ever collect a testimonial by working down this list.

Use the strongest element you can muster first, then stack more.

### Results-oriented testimonials (the gold standard)

- Capture an honest "I used it, got this result" quote.
- Be extra careful in **health, finance, and investing**; attach the required disclaimers and never fabricate a results claim.

### Company or personal testimonials

- Ask current customers or colleagues: "What would you tell a friend about working with us? May we quote that?"
- These are easy to get. Just ask.

### Celebrity or authority endorsements

- Send your product to recognized names in your niche and ask for a few words.
- You only need one or two yeses to change everything. Most will ignore you; ask anyway.

### Statistics and data points

- Search your topic plus "statistics" or "study" (for example, "for sale by owner statistics").
- Cite the source, then frame the stat: "Nine out of ten for-sale-by-owners fail within thirty days. Don't let that be you."

### Expert quotes

- Find quotes that support the action you want the reader to take.
- Drop them in where you need to reinforce the buying decision.

### Before and after demonstrations

- Show the transformation: what life looked like before, and what it looks like after.
- Use screenshots, photos, screen recordings, or a side-by-side narrative.

### Free samples and trials

- Hand the product to real users and ask for an honest testimonial in return.
- This is the fastest legitimate way to generate your first results-oriented testimonials.

### Third-party research citations

- Reference independent studies, industry reports, or government data.
- Name the source so the claim carries their authority, not just yours.

### Reasons to believe list

- Build a bulleted list of concrete, checkable facts that make your promise credible.
- Mix the sources: a statistic, an endorsement, a guarantee, a case study, a demonstration.

> Fastest path to your first testimonial: give the product away, ask for feedback, and ask permission to quote it.

## Stealth close techniques

Most readers keep their anti-sale radar on full blast. A stealth close (the "Columbo close") flies under that radar by delivering value first, then pivoting to a soft offer that looks identical to the surrounding content. Reserve stealth closes for free content; use a direct call to action on actual sales pages.

Every stealth close has two moves:

1. **Deliver value** (teach, entertain, or share a "didja know" tip).
2. **Pivot** to a soft invitation tied to a benefit.

### The "by the way" close

Open the pivot with "By the way, did you know..." and state a true benefit:

- "By the way, did you know [product] will help you [benefit]? It's true. Go here for a quick demo."
- "By the way, did you know [product] is on sale this week? It's true. Go here to grab one."

Swap in variations so the pattern stays invisible: "Oh, before I forget...", "One more thing...", "Did you realize...".

### The recommendation close

Position the offer as a personal recommendation, not a pitch:

- "If I were starting over today, the one tool I would grab first is [product]. Here's why."
- "My go-to for [task] is [product]. Worth every penny."

### The story close (let the story do the selling)

Tell the outcome story, then let the reader connect the dots:

- "Last month I used [product] to get [result]. The whole thing took [short time]."
- End with a single line pointing to where they can do the same.

### The educational close (teach first, offer second)

Teach a tip that creates the need, then point to the tool that fills it:

- "Here's a ten-minute way to [solve the problem]. By the way, [product] automates the whole thing. Demo here."

### The question close

Ask a question that surfaces desire, then answer it with the offer:

- "Ever wish you could [benefit]? That's exactly what [product] does. See how here."

### Where to use stealth closes

Email teasers and P.S. lines, blog posts, articles, Kindle and e-books, Facebook, YouTube, and Pinterest content, and the end of any live video. Never reformat the close to stand out; anything that screams "ad" blows the cover.

## Ethical persuasion principles

Persuasion channels a desire that already exists. Manipulation manufactures a false one. Stay on the right side of the line.

### Meet people in their pain, then solve it

The good way to "go negative" is to name a real problem, fear, or frustration so you get in sync with the reader. Until they feel understood, they will not listen to your solution. Once in sync, demonstrate how your product prevents the mistake, calms the fear, or defeats the enemy.

### Never attack a person or company by name

You may attack a **behavior, a result, or a method**. Never name names. Calling people out backfires and burns bridges.

### Compare without targeting

- "Some competitors will tell you [claim], but that's just not true. Here are the facts."
- "You may have noticed some people charge extra for [X]. We don't think that's right. Here's why."

### Sell things that actually help people

Channel an existing desire onto a product that genuinely serves the buyer. If the product does not deliver the promised outcome, no copy technique rescues the relationship.

### Never use false scarcity or fake urgency

Time deadlines and limited quantities only work if you honor them. Extending a "one-time" deadline or restocking "limited" inventory destroys your integrity and trains buyers to wait you out. Real urgency comes from real bonuses, real deadlines, and real limits.

### Honesty builds long-term customers

Never manufacture testimonials or results claims. Trust compounds; a single lie resets it to zero. Repeat customers come from feeling respected, not pressured.

## When to hire a copywriter vs DIY

### Default to DIY

Nobody knows your product and market as well as you do. A hired writer needs hours of interviews just to learn what you already carry in your head, and what you get back is still a first draft you must tweak. Learn the skill so you can create copy fast, stay independent, and recognize good work.

### Hire when

- You genuinely lack the time and the business is already earning.
- You want an outside perspective to critique your draft.
- You need a first draft to react to and refine.

### How to hire well

- **Audition on a small job.** Test three or four writers on an email teaser, a headline set, or an intro paragraph before committing to a big project. Never hand a stranger five figures up front.
- **Ask for work samples and verify authorship.** Cheap writers often subcontract to juniors; confirm they actually wrote what they show you.
- **Call past clients.** "Did so-and-so write this? How was the process? What were the results?"
- **Check for boilerplate.** Paste their copy into a plagiarism checker; if the same letter appears across many sites with names swapped, walk away.
- **Match the writer to the job size.** Vetting someone for a single email is different from vetting someone for a long-form sales letter.

### Expect to still work

Whatever you get back is version one. You will edit, mold, and test it. When you add up all the work you still do after hiring, doing it yourself is often the faster path.
