# Copy Review Checklist

> Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.

Score each of the 12 dimensions: **0** (missing), **1** (weak), or **2** (strong). Maximum total is 24. Worked-on copy that scores below 16 needs another pass before it ships.

## How To Use This Checklist

1. Read the copy end to end once, as a prospect would.
2. Score each dimension using the diagnostic questions below.
3. Add the scores and compare against the quality bands.
4. Fix the lowest-scoring dimensions first, then re-score.
5. When a dimension moves from 0 or 1 to 2, A/B test the improved version against the original. Never change more than one variable at a time.

## The 12 Dimensions

### 1. Headline Strength

The headline determines whether anyone reads further. Treat it as roughly 80% of the copy's success.

Diagnostic questions:

- Does the headline target a specific fear or desire?
- Would the ideal prospect stop scrolling to read it?
- Is the timeframe or number believable (not hype)?

Common failure: a headline about the writer ("How I Went From Bankrupt To Successful") instead of about the reader's desired result ("How To Gain An Unfair Advantage").

### 2. F.R.E.D. Alignment

The copy must speak to the avatar's specific fears, desires, and objections. Generic copy aimed at "everyone" converts no one.

Diagnostic questions:

- Does the copy name the prospect's specific problem in their own words?
- Does it address their biggest fear about failing again?
- Does it match the language and identity of the target group?

Common failure: describing features the audience does not care about instead of the outcome they crave.

### 3. Bullet Quality

Bullets carry the weight of the copy. Each should surface a benefit and its meaning, not just a feature.

Diagnostic questions:

- Does every bullet follow the "feature so you can benefit, which means meaning" structure?
- Do the bullets build curiosity and pressure to act?
- Are there four to twelve excellent bullets rather than a long list of mediocre ones?

Common failure: a spec-sheet list ("18-volt, one-inch bit") with no translation into what the buyer gets or how it makes them feel.

### 4. Emotional Connection

People decide on emotion and justify with logic. Lead with feeling; support with facts.

Diagnostic questions:

- Does the copy make the reader feel the pain of the problem before pitching the solution?
- Does it paint a picture of life after the desired result?
- Are the stakes (what they lose by not acting) emotionally clear?

Common failure: a wall of statistics and specs with no story or felt experience.

### 5. Clarity

If a 12-year-old cannot understand the offer, the copy is too clever.

Diagnostic questions:

- Can the reader state in one sentence what you are selling and what it does for them?
- Are sentences short, with no jargon or inside-baseball terms?
- Is there exactly one offer, or is the reader choosing between multiple paths?

Common failure: burying the offer under qualifications, options, and tangents.

### 6. Call To Action

The CTA must be clear, specific, and singular. Tell the reader exactly what to do next.

Diagnostic questions:

- Is there one and only one action to take?
- Does the CTA use a specific verb ("Click here to start your 7-day trial") rather than vague phrasing ("Learn more")?
- Is it obvious what happens after they click?

Common failure: multiple competing buttons or a passive "if you're interested, you can..." framing.

### 7. Proof And Credibility

Claims need support. Show, do not assert.

Diagnostic questions:

- Are there specific testimonials with names and results?
- Are there concrete numbers, case studies, or demonstrations?
- Does the source of authority (experience, endorsements, data) come through?

Common failure: unsubstantiated superlatives ("the best", "world-class") with zero evidence.

### 8. Risk Reversal

Remove the buyer's fear of making a mistake. The less they have to lose, the more likely they act.

Diagnostic questions:

- Is there a clear guarantee, and is the terms specific ("30-day, no-questions-asked")?
- Does the copy handle the top objections explicitly?
- Is there a trial, sample, or free first step?

Common failure: asking for full commitment with no guarantee and no acknowledged objection.

### 9. Urgency Or Scarcity

Give the reader a reason to act now rather than later. "Later" usually means "never."

Diagnostic questions:

- Is there a credible deadline or limited-quantity reason?
- Is the urgency real (bonuses expiring, seats filling) rather than manufactured hype?
- Is the consequence of waiting stated?

Common failure: a page with no time or quantity pressure, letting the prospect bookmark-and-forget.

### 10. Formatting

Copy must be scannable. People skim before they read.

Diagnostic questions:

- Are paragraphs short (one to three sentences)?
- Are key phrases bolded so a skimmer still gets the message?
- Do bullets and subheads break up the page visually?

Common failure: dense blocks of unbroken text that look like work to read.

### 11. Tone Match

Write like you talk to a friend, not like a corporation. Conversational copy outperforms formal copy.

Diagnostic questions:

- Does it sound like one person talking to another?
- Is the reading level conversational, not academic?
- Have you read it aloud without tripping over the sentences?

Common failure: corporate voice, passive constructions, and jargon that create distance instead of rapport.

### 12. Focus

One message, one audience, one action. Diluted copy converts poorly.

Diagnostic questions:

- Does the entire piece serve a single big idea?
- Is every paragraph aimed at the same reader?
- Has anything that serves the writer's ego rather than the reader's need been cut?

Common failure: trying to speak to multiple audiences or pitch multiple products in one piece, splitting attention and conversion.

## Scoring Guide

Score each dimension 0, 1, or 2:

- **0** = missing. The dimension is absent or actively harmful.
- **1** = weak. It is present but generic, vague, or underdeveloped.
- **2** = strong. It is specific, audience-matched, and would survive an A/B test as the champion.

### Quality Bands

| Total (out of 24) | Band | Action |
|-------------------|------|--------|
| 22 to 24 | Excellent | Ship it, then set up an A/B test to try to beat it. |
| 18 to 21 | Good | Fix the 0s and 1s before publishing. Close to ready. |
| 13 to 17 | Fair | Needs another full pass. Do not ship as primary copy. |
| 0 to 12 | Poor | Rewrite from the headline down. Start over on the weakest dimensions. |

## A/B Testing Discipline

Once copy scores well, keep improving it. Small gains compound dramatically: moving a conversion rate from 1% to 2% on a $100 product with $90 traffic cost takes profit from $10 to $170 per hundred visitors, a 700% increase.

Rules:

- Test only one variable at a time (headline, price, button text, bullet, image). Changing more than one invalidates the result.
- Test for measurable actions: purchases, clicks, subscriptions, sign-ups.
- If the challenger beats the champion, the challenger becomes the new control. Then try to beat it again.
- Automate testing with a built-in tool. Do not run split tests by hand.
