# Sales Formulas Reference

> Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.

## The Three Core Sales Formulas

Three formulas carry virtually any sales message: a 20-page sales letter, a one-minute video, a commercial, or a 10-minute talk. Before applying any of them, understand your target reader's problems, questions, roadblocks, and desired results. One to two hours of research up front pays off for the rest of your life.

---

## Formula 1 - Problem, Agitate, Solve (PAS)

PAS is the most versatile formula. Use it for anything, anywhere. It works especially well with cold traffic.

### PAS: the three parts

1. **State the problem.** Name the exact problem they face in no uncertain terms.
2. **Agitate it.** Make it worse. Make it hurt. Twist the knife. Agitation is the magic of this formula. Do not leave the problem alone after stating it.
3. **Introduce the solution.** Present your product or service as the answer with a transition like "Luckily for you, there's now a solution."

### PAS template

```text
[Problem]: Here's the problem. If you [situation], this is something you need to get under control quickly.

[Agitate]: If you don't, you could [consequence 1]. You could [consequence 2]. You could carry [burden] for the rest of your life.

[Solution]: Luckily for you, there's now a solution. [Product name] helps you [benefit 1], [benefit 2], and [benefit 3].
```

### Worked example (dog aggression)

- **Problem:** "If your dog barks or acts like it's going to bite other people, this is something you need to get under control quickly."
- **Agitate:** "If you don't, you could be subject to a lawsuit. Your dog could injure a child. You could carry the guilt and the financial burden of a single attack for the rest of your life."
- **Solution:** "Luckily for you, there's now a solution. The Complete A-to-Z Puppy-Dog Training Guide helps you with aggression, potty training, tricks, and socializing your dog to be a happy member of the family."

### Agitate patterns

Use a "which means you" statement to pour on the pain. This is the same device used to evoke positive feeling in bullets, inverted here to crush.

- "Which means you'll never get a chance to [dream]."
- "Which means you'll struggle with [pain] for the rest of your life."
- "Which means your [message/dream] dies with you."

The goal is for the reader to say, "That hurts. Save me now." One or two sharp agitate statements are enough. It is a one-two punch, not a beating.

## Formula 2 - The Benefit Formula

A positive, desire-led message. Use this when you focus on what they want rather than a problem. It works well with hot and warm traffic.

### The pattern

Lead with benefit, benefit, benefit, then the action you want them to take.

### Benefit formula template

```text
If you want [benefit 1], if you want [benefit 2], or you simply want [benefit 3], then you need to check out [product name]. Here's why...
```

### Worked examples

- Dog training: "If you want to teach your dog some great tricks, or just have a lot more fun with your pet, then you need to check out The 10 Tricks You Can Teach Your Dog In A Weekend course. Here's why..."
- Real estate: "If you want to find deals before anybody else and create a steady stream of new deals you don't have to chase, then you need to check out Flippers' Paradise. Here's why..."
- Relationships: "If you want to reignite the fire with your spouse or simply reconnect with your best friend, then check out Marriage Revitalization Secrets. Here's why..."

## Formula 3 - Before, After, Bridge

This formula uses NLP future pacing. Paint the current pain, show the transformed state, then bridge with your product. It works with all traffic temperatures.

### Before, after, bridge: the three parts

1. **Before.** Describe the way things are now. There is a problem, a question, a roadblock, or something making them unhappy.
2. **After.** Tell them to imagine their life once the problem is gone. Future pace: "Imagine your life once [negative] is gone." Paint the picture of them happy, the problem solved.
3. **Bridge.** Present the path from where they are to where they want to be. "Here's how you get there."

### Before, after, bridge template

```text
[Before]: Right now you [current pain]. You [frustration]. You [roadblock]. But [what makes it worse].

[After]: Now imagine what it would be like to [desired state]. You [positive feeling]. You [positive outcome]. You're [emotion].

[Bridge]: Here's how you get to that point. [Product name] helps you get [benefit 1], [benefit 2], and [benefit 3].
```

### Worked example (dog training)

- **Before:** "Your dog's not listening. You're worried he'll get loose, run into traffic, get in a fight, or bite a neighbor's child."
- **After:** "Now imagine your dog listening to you, not because he's scared but because he loves you. He walks with you without a leash. You enjoy your time together and you're never worried about aggression."
- **Bridge:** "Here's how you get to that point. Dog Training Secrets helps you get [benefit], [benefit], and [benefit]."

## Where to Use the Formulas

These formulas work anywhere you want to start the sales process:

- Tweets and social media posts
- Blog posts
- Email teasers
- Video sales letters
- Sales pages and direct mail
- Facebook Live and webinars

Test each formula to find the one that works best with your audience.

## The 13-Step Sales Letter Template

A sales letter is a series of stacked parts, like stepping stones across a stream. The job of each part is to pull the reader to the next part. Miss one and the reader stumbles, gets wet, and never reaches the buy button. Do thirteen small steps in turn rather than one giant project.

This works for a 10-page letter, a one-page direct mail piece, a web page, or a video. Each step can be a page, a paragraph, a sentence, or a few words. Hit all the points in order.

### 1. Headline package

The headline package grabs attention from the right people. It can consist of a pre-headline, headline, and sub-headline.

- **Pre-headline:** Calls out the audience. Example: "Attn: All Funnel Hackers"
- **Headline:** The big promise. Example: "How To Get ALL Of Your Sales Letters Written (In Under 10 Minutes) WITHOUT Hiring An Expensive Copywriter!"
- **Sub-headline:** Reinforces or qualifies. Example: "Works Even If You HATE Writing And Never Want To Know ANYTHING About Copywriting!"

For a written letter, add a "From the desk of [your name]" line and a "Re: [topic]" line. For a video, the first words out of your mouth are the headline: grab attention, identify yourself, state what to expect.

### 2. Shocking statement

Most people read in a distracted, hypnotic state. Shock them so they stop and pay attention. Use a "Did you know?" statement that goes against something they believe or exposes something they suspect is true.

- "Did you know most people who try to write a sales letter fail miserably?"
- "Did you know 99% of people who start writing a book never finish?"

The desired reaction: "Oh crap. I better pay attention." Mine your research for statistics that surprise.

### 3. Define the problem

State the exact problem they face. Use the PAS formula (Formula 1 above). Open with: "Here's the problem you face:" and fill in the blank.

- "Here's the problem you face: As a society we've become so sedentary that young people don't know how to get into shape."
- "Here's the problem you face: Most people think writing a book is so hard they can't imagine becoming an author."

### 4. Agitate

Make it hurt. The more it hurts, the more they need a solution. If you leave it at the problem stage, they say "I'm not that bad off" and go back to the couch. Pour on the pain with "which means you" statements.

- "Which means you'll never serve your country."
- "Which means your message dies with you."

Do not skip this step. They don't buy if it don't hurt.

### 5. Present the solution

Introduce your product as the answer.

- "Luckily for you, there's now a solution. Let me introduce you to [product]."
- "Let me introduce you to my new coaching program."
- "Let me introduce you to a piece of software that writes all your sales copy for you."

### 6. Bullets to arouse curiosity

Turn your features, benefits, and meanings into bullets that build curiosity and desire. Use the Ultimate Bullet Formula (feature + benefit + "which means" meaning). Aim for six to ten rock-solid bullets. Ten excellent bullets pull the weight of fifty mediocre ones.

### 7. Credibility statement

Answer the question "Why you?" Tell people why you are qualified to bring this solution. This can be one sentence or a full page, depending on the price and the weight the copy carries.

- For a high-ticket item: list education, qualifications, and experience.
- For a mass product: one line can carry it. The inventor of The Perfect PushUp sold $100 million of fitness equipment with five words: "Invented by a Navy SEAL."

### 8. Proof

Answer the question "Why should I believe you?" Use testimonials, endorsements, statistics, quotes, studies, screenshots, before-and-after images, and bank statements. Open with: "But don't just take my word for it. Take a look at this."

Test every claim: if someone said "Prove this in a court of law," could you? Always tell the truth and back it up.

### 9. Sum up the offer and give price

Tell them exactly what they get, when they get it, how they get it, and how much it costs.

- What: the product, components, and deliverables
- When: instantly, weekly, in 3 to 4 days
- How: digital download, UPS, live coaching
- How much: the price, framed as a deal

Frame the price as value pricing, not commodity pricing. A reasonable discount (10 to 30 percent) feels like a deal; a wild drop ("regularly $399, now $2.50") trips the BS detector. Those who live by the price die by the price.

### 10. Bonuses and pot sweeteners

Add value to push the offer over the top. Build up the value of each bonus and show why it is a valuable addition.

The core principle: sell dollars for dimes. Pile on bonuses until the total value of the offer is roughly 10 times the price you charge. A powerful tactic is to take your competitor's unique selling proposition and turn it into a free bonus, so buyers get everything they want without buying from anyone else.

Do not pile on crap bonuses for the sake of bulk. Be strategic. Create an offer that makes so much sense people would be insane to say no.

### 11. Guarantee

Take away the risk. This can be a single sentence ("30-Day No Questions Asked Guarantee") or a paragraph that restates every benefit you sold them on.

```text
Not only do we guarantee this unconditionally for 30 days, but if it doesn't show you [benefit 1], if it doesn't [benefit 2], if it doesn't [benefit 3], we don't want your money. We'll give it all back. No questions asked. No hard feelings.
```

### 12. Call to action

Tell them exactly what to do. "Click here to buy now." Give them another reason to buy right now ("When you order today, we'll give you another 10% off"). For a long letter, recap everything they get in short bullets.

### 13. Postscript (P.S.)

Restate the main benefits, the reason to act now, and tell them to buy again. The P.S. is often the most-read part of a letter.

```text
P.S. This gem will sell for $49. This introductory price of $29 is a fast offer, so act now before it's gone.

P.P.S. Let's be blunt: If you pass on this offer, in a week from today will you have [result] or not? Probably not! Get this now and have [result] as fast as one week from today. Your satisfaction is guaranteed. Click here!
```

### Summary table

| Step | Purpose | Job |
|------|---------|-----|
| 1. Headline package | Grab the right attention | Stop the reader |
| 2. Shocking statement | Break the hypnotic state | Make them pay attention |
| 3. Define the problem | Name the pain | Set up the need |
| 4. Agitate | Make it hurt | Create urgency to solve |
| 5. Present the solution | Introduce the product | Offer the relief |
| 6. Bullets | Arouse curiosity | Build desire |
| 7. Credibility | Answer "Why you?" | Earn the right to sell |
| 8. Proof | Answer "Why believe you?" | Remove doubt |
| 9. Offer and price | Make the deal clear | State what, when, how, how much |
| 10. Bonuses | Sweeten the pot | Stack value above price |
| 11. Guarantee | Remove risk | Make saying yes safe |
| 12. Call to action | Tell them to buy | Direct the next step |
| 13. Postscript | Restate and push | Catch the skimmers |

## First Draft Methodology

The hardest draft you will ever write is the first draft. There is never a perfect time. The blinking cursor on a blank page is the enemy.

### Think in chunks, not wholes

Do not think "I have to write a sales letter." Think through the chunks. A sales letter is a headline package, a sales story, some bullets, a guarantee, and a call to action. An email is a subject line, a salutation, a PAS formula, and a link. A video is a grabber, a PAS, three bullets, reasons to act, and a call to action.

Writing thirteen small steps is far more attainable than writing one giant letter. Spread the chunks across sessions if you are a part-time entrepreneur.

### Get the first draft done fast

Your number one job is to get the first draft or version done as fast as you can. This is the key to success.

### Use your swipe file

Do not stare at a blank sheet. Use your swipe file to stimulate your creativity. Adapt sales letters, headlines, bullets, and calls to action that already work. Your swipe file provides models for copy that converts.

### Edit, do not create

Once you have something on paper, editing is a hundred times easier than writing from scratch. Seeing it on screen lets your mind say "I should add this, cut that, move this here, we need a guarantee, we should show a picture here." You cannot edit a blank page.

### The workflow

1. Research your audience (1 to 2 hours).
2. Break the project into chunks using the 13-step template.
3. Draft each chunk fast and ugly. Do not polish yet.
4. Pull from your swipe file when you stall.
5. Finish the complete first draft.
6. Run the polish pass on the whole (see swipe-and-polish.md).
