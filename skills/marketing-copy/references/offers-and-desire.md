# Offers and Desire Reference

> Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.

## Desire Versus Selling

You can lead a horse to water, but you cannot make him drink. You can, however, make him thirsty. Stories make people thirsty; sales copy tells them where to buy a drink. Build desire with content and stories, then point to the offer with sales copy. Use one to set up the other.

---

## Desire Building Techniques

Desire is created before the price is ever revealed. The goal is to make people want what you sell without giving away so much that they no longer need to buy.

### The four types of stories

There is no difference between the stories you use in content marketing and the stories you use on sales letters and in videos. Only your intention differs.

1. **Actual stories** about your business, other people, or anything that illustrates your point.
2. **Case studies** (the hero's journey): "I was here and unhappy. I did this with the product. These are the results I got."
3. **Examples** showing how something works, how it was applied, and the results it produced.
4. **The Three Ms of Content** (see below).

### The Three Ms of Content

The Three Ms create content that does not fully solve the problem yet creates the need for what you sell and increases urgency to buy now.

- **Myths.** Dispel an untruth people believe. Whole books and webinars are built around debunking myths.
- **Misconceptions.** Clear up incorrect ideas and replace them with correct understanding.
- **Mistakes.** Point out where people go wrong. Mistakes are the most powerful of the three because nobody wants to look stupid. Since childhood we have feared mistakes; as adults we will do anything to avoid them.

Create stories around myths, misconceptions, and mistakes without giving away the solution inside your product. People will feel like you have been spilling the beans all day, yet still need to buy.

### Future pacing

Tell the story of what their life will be like when they take action. Paint pictures and emotions without teaching the how.

```text
I want you to imagine having [desired outcome]. You [visual detail]. You [emotional detail]. Now imagine [specific scenario where the outcome pays off]. Think about what your life would be like when you have [result]. How will this boost your [credibility/business/confidence]? How will you feel about yourself?
```

You have not taught them how to get there. You have only made them thirsty to get there.

### Four ways to sell when creating content

These methods let you give away a ton of value without giving away anything that prevents people from buying.

1. **Give something valuable for free that leads to a purchase.** The free thing naturally creates the need for the paid thing. Free boat, then sell the trailer and motor. Free riding lessons and saddle, then sell the horse. The free content is so good that people think "the next logical step is to buy what they sell."
2. **Tell them what and why, then sell them how.** Lay out the exact steps to achieve a result and why it matters. Then offer the tool that does the steps for them.
3. **Teach the steps leading up to the purchase.** Teach how to build the system; the only missing piece is your tool. Teach recipes using your supplement; they get thirsty watching and go buy it.
4. **Teach the hard way, sell the easy button.** Teach the manual method in full detail, then offer the tool that automates it. This works so well it feels like cheating: teach how to dig a well with a shovel, then offer the backhoe.

## The "Oh Damn, I Gotta Have That" Formula

Irresistible desire comes from a four-part promise. The promise, usually carried in the headline, is the number one key to selling. Hit all four parts to check every box in the prospect's decision process.

### Part 1 - The Hurdle

Address the action they believe they must take to get what they want. What do they see as the hard thing? Pay attention to the action words (verbs) your audience uses. People visualize actions best because actions involve motion, and motion dominates how the brain processes.

Frame it as "How to [action verb]": how to get, how to write, how to create, how to lose, how to train.

- How to drive a golf ball (not "hit" or "strike" unless that is their word)
- How to lose 20 pounds
- How to train your dog

### Part 2 - The Prize

The prize is the result they want from taking the action. Pay attention to the result keywords your niche uses.

- Your own outrageously profitable e-book and fat royalty checks
- A passionate relationship
- Freedom from back pain
- Results like a seasoned pro (or like a specific hero they recognize: Tiger Woods, Arnold Palmer)

### Part 3 - The Timing

Answer "When will I get it?" Everyone is a highly-functioning five-year-old wanting to know when Santa arrives. Give a timeframe they can wrap their head around. It should be almost unbelievable but within the realm of possibility, so they can only blame themselves if they do not do it.

Two ways to present timing:

- **Time for them to do it:** "Write a 100-page book in about a week."
- **Time for you to teach it:** "How to improve your golf swing in two 30-minute sessions." "Give me 17 minutes and I'll show you how to start a conversation with the most beautiful woman in the room."

### Part 4 - The Eliminator

Let them off the hook. It is not their fault they do not have what they want. Everyone has mental barriers: "It takes too long," "I don't know what to do," "It costs too much." If you do not eliminate the excuse, you lose the sale.

Never tell them it is their fault. Ever. Reframe the blame outward, then redirect to a better strategy.

- "Even if you can't write, can't type, and failed high school English class."
- "It's not your fault because you've been lied to about how carbs and proteins interact."

Use the transitions "even if" or "without" to sweep away the obstacle.

### Assembled template

```text
How to [action/hurdle] [prize/result] in as little as [timing] even if [eliminator].
```

### Worked examples

- "How to write and publish your own outrageously profitable e-book in as little as seven days, even if you can't write, can't type, and failed high school English class."
- "How to use online dating sites to find the love of your life in 30 days, even if you've failed at online dating before."
- "How to get your spouse talking with you again and save your relationship in one 15-minute session, even if everything you've tried in the past didn't work."

### Promise superchargers

Add these to amplify the formula.

- **Money promise:** "Make as much as an extra $100 a day."
- **Forgive past failure:** "Even if you've tried before and failed."
- **Tighter timeline:** "In 60 minutes or less." "In less than a week."
- **Cost modifier:** "For less than the cost of a cup of coffee at Starbucks." "For less than a medium everything pizza every month."

The cost modifier creates the reaction: "Oh damn, for less than a monthly pizza I can have this? Heck yeah."

## Bonus Stacking Methodology

Bonuses turn a good offer into an offer people would be insane to refuse. The principle is selling dollars for dimes.

### The core ratio

In the prospect's mind, the total value of your offer plus bonuses must feel worth roughly 10 times the price you charge.

- Charge $97, deliver $1,000 in demonstrable value.
- Charge $1,000, deliver $10,000 in value.

The exact ratio can be 5-to-1 or 20-to-1, but it must feel like an authentic, real deal. If somebody offered to sell you as many dollars as you want for 10 cents each, you would take as many as you could get. That is the feeling to create.

### The competitor-bonus tactic

Take your competitor's unique selling proposition and turn it into a free bonus with your offer. Buyers compare you against competitors. When they see they get everything they want from you without buying from anyone else, they buy from you. This single tactic can transform a flat offer.

### Stacking discipline

Do not pile on crap bonuses for the sake of bulk. Be strategic. Use bonuses to create an offer that makes so much sense and feels so right that people would be insane to say no.

## Reframing Features as Benefits

Features do not sell. Benefits sell. The deepest selling happens when you surface the meaning under the benefit. This is the "lipstick on the pig" principle: transform boring features into exciting benefits that connect to how the reader feels.

### The three-level distinction

| Level | Definition | Example (cordless drill) |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Feature | What it IS | 18-volt battery |
| Benefit | What it DOES | Drill through hardwood without recharging every five minutes |
| Meaning | How it makes them FEEL | Finish the honey-do list in one afternoon instead of three weekends |

### The formula

```text
It [feature] so you can [benefit], which means [meaning].
```

The "which means" clause is the secret sauce. It turns a spec sheet into a desire-building machine.

### When copy underperforms, check the bullets

If bullets read like a technical manual, they are featuring features, not benefits. Rewrite each bullet so it highlights the payoff and the meaning. Bullets build the desire that drives people to enter their credit card and buy.

## Risk Reversal Patterns

People hesitate because they fear losing money on a bad purchase. Remove that risk and conversion rises.

### The unconditional guarantee

A single sentence can carry it: "30-Day No Questions Asked Guarantee."

### The benefit-restating guarantee

Restate every benefit you sold them on as a condition for keeping their money.

```text
Not only do we guarantee this unconditionally for 30 days, but if it doesn't show you [benefit 1], if it doesn't [benefit 2], if it doesn't [benefit 3], we don't want your money. We'll give it all back. No questions asked. No hard feelings.
```

### "Keep the bonuses" technique

Let buyers keep the bonuses even if they refund. This makes the risk reversal feel generous rather than defensive and gives them a reason to feel good about trying.

### Sizing the guarantee to the price

For a low-ticket item, one line is enough. For a high-ticket item where the copy carries the entire weight of the decision, expand the guarantee into a full paragraph that addresses every objection.

## Price Anchoring

How you present price after stacking value determines whether the price feels like a steal or a rip-off.

### Present value before price

Build the value stack first, then reveal the price. The reader should feel they are getting far more than they pay before they ever see a number.

### Reasonable discounts feel real

A 10 to 30 percent discount off a regular price feels like a deal. A wild drop ("regularly $399, now $2.50") trips the BS detector and turns people off. Model Amazon: crossed-out regular price, modest discount, clear savings.

### Price can be too low, not just too high

A $97 options course kills it with a work-at-home audience. The same price makes experienced investors laugh and assume it is worthless. Match the price to the audience's expectations. Test to find the sweet spot.

### Value pricing over commodity pricing

Those who live by the price die by the price. If the only reason to buy is a low price, you rarely make money. Provide value pricing. The cheapest seller in town rarely profits unless they have a strong back-end funnel.

## Urgency and Reasons to Buy Now

A great offer with no urgency gets postponed indefinitely. Give a clear reason to buy right now.

### The three classic urgency levers (use with integrity)

- **Bonuses.** Stack added value available only if they buy now. The safest lever because it never forces you to close the offer.
- **Time deadline.** Set a date and shut down. Dangerous: if you re-open "just for 24 hours for my people," you destroy your integrity and train buyers to wait. If you set a deadline, honor it permanently.
- **Limited quantities.** When you run out, stop. If you magically restock, buyers who acted early realize you lied.

### The durable lever: fear of losing out

Instead of artificial deadlines or fake scarcity, use pacing. Lay out what happens if they do not act.

```text
If you don't do this, here's what happens. You won't be able to [opportunity]. You won't have [advantage]. You'll stay stuck where you are while [competitor/peer] passes you by.
```

Stack three to five reasons why waiting costs them. This works better and longer than time deadlines because it never forces you to compromise integrity.

### The emotional driver

Fear, desire, curiosity, pain. Copy must contain an emotional driver. Nine times out of ten, people buy out of dissatisfaction with current circumstances. Until it hurts enough to change, people do not change. Infuse dissatisfaction into the copy to create the drive to act now.

## Offer Structure Template

Assemble the full offer in this order.

```text
1. MAIN PRODUCT
   What it is, what it does, the primary result it delivers.

2. BONUSES (value stack)
   Bonus 1 - [name] - [value]
   Bonus 2 - [name] - [value]
   Bonus 3 - [name] - [value]
   Bonus 4 - [name] - [value]
   (Aim for total value at least 10x the price.)

3. TOTAL VALUE: $[sum of all values]

4. YOUR PRICE: $[price] (a fraction of the value)

5. GUARANTEE
   [Risk reversal: money-back, keep the bonuses, no questions asked]

6. URGENCY
   [Reason to buy now: bonuses expire, fear of losing out, pacing]

7. CALL TO ACTION
   [Click here to get it now]
```

## The Value Stack Table

Present the offer as a table so the reader can see how much value they receive versus what they pay.

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| [Main product name] | $[value] |
| [Bonus 1 name] | $[value] |
| [Bonus 2 name] | $[value] |
| [Bonus 3 name] | $[value] |
| [Bonus 4 name] | $[value] |
| **Total Value** | **$[sum]** |
| **Your Price Today** | **$[price]** |

### Worked example

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| The 7-Day E-Book Course | $197 |
| Fast-Start Video Training | $97 |
| Fill-in-the-Blank Headline Swipe File | $67 |
| Personal Cover Design Review | $197 |
| 30-Day Email Support | $297 |
| **Total Value** | **$855** |
| **Your Price Today** | **$97** |

The reader sees $855 of value for $97. That is selling dollars for dimes.

## Quick Audit - Is the Offer Irresistible?

Run this checklist on any offer before publishing.

- [ ] Is the headline about them, not about you?
- [ ] Is there a bold benefit or promise that attracts the right people?
- [ ] Does the buyer understand exactly what they get, how they get it, and when?
- [ ] Is the price clear and easy to find?
- [ ] Does the total value stack feel like at least 10 times the price?
- [ ] Are bullets benefit- and meaning-driven, not feature lists?
- [ ] Is there a guarantee that removes the risk?
- [ ] Is there a genuine reason to buy now?
- [ ] Is there an emotional driver (most often dissatisfaction with the status quo)?
- [ ] Can you substantiate every claim you make?

If the answer to any is no, fix it before the copy goes live.
