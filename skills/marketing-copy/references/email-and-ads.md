# Email and Ads Reference

> Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.

## The One Rule for Email and Ads

Both email teasers and online ads share a single purpose: get the right person to click. The email or ad does not make the sale. The sales page or video does that. Your job is to stop the right person, build curiosity, and drive one click. The wrong person should keep scrolling.

---

## Email Teasers

### The Email Teaser Pattern

Every email teaser follows six moves. Keep the whole email to 3 to 5 sentences after the salutation.

1. **Subject line** - earn the open (see templates below).
2. **Salutation** - acknowledge the reader by name. If you cannot personalize, use a group label ("Hey everyone," "Hi Fellow [Group]").
3. **Shocking statement** - pop the reader out of autopilot. State something unexpected, surprising, or counterintuitive.
4. **Build curiosity** - use 2 to 4 bullets or 2 to 3 sentences that amplify the benefit without revealing the solution.
5. **Specific call to action** - tell them exactly what to do: "Click here to see it."
6. **Close** - sign off like a friend: "I'll see you over there. Thanks, [Name]."

### Subject Line Patterns and Templates

Short, concise subject lines win. Questions outperform statements. The subject line should attract the right buyer and repel the non-buyer.

### Curiosity

- This blew me away
- This was totally unexpected
- You've got to check this out

### Question (pain-awareness)

- Worried about failing your next [event]?
- Failed your last [event]?
- [Event] coming up?

### Question (desire-awareness)

- Want to be a published author?
- Would you like to [big payoff]?
- Have you ever wanted to [desired result]?

### Benefit

- How to [result] in [timeframe]
- The [number] [topic] secrets every [audience] needs

### News

- Got an exciting new announcement
- Cool new free [webinar/guide] on [topic]

### Story

- The story of a [unlikely character] who [surprising result]

### Preview Text Optimization

The preview text is the second subject line. It appears beside or below the subject in most inboxes.

- Treat it as a curiosity bridge, not a repeat of the subject.
- Complement the subject line rather than echo it.
- Keep it under 100 characters so mobile clients show it whole.
- Use it to deepen the open loop: subject raises the question, preview hints at the answer behind the click.

### Email Body Structure

Write like you are emailing one friend, not broadcasting to a list. Most readers see email on mobile, so short wins.

- **Hook (1 sentence):** shocking statement or question.
- **Build curiosity (2 to 4 bullets or sentences):** amplify the payoff without giving away the solution.
- **Link and CTA (1 sentence):** one specific instruction. One link. Nothing else competes for the click.

### How to Write Email Sequences

Apply the teaser pattern across a multi-day arc. Each email stands alone but the sequence compounds curiosity.

- **Day 1 - Pattern interrupt:** introduce the problem or big promise. Subject raises the question; body teases the answer behind the click.
- **Day 2 - Story and proof:** tell the origin story or a transformation. Use a story-type hook. Build curiosity around how it happened.
- **Day 3 - The mistake:** expose the common approach that fails. Use a warning hook. Position your angle as the overlooked alternative.
- **Day 4 - The mechanism:** reveal the unique method or "how." Curiosity shifts from "what is it" to "how does it work."
- **Day 5 - Call to action and deadline:** state the offer, the guarantee, and a reason to act now. Benefit subject line with urgency in the preview.

Friends do not send friends ten-page sales letters in email. If you must err, go shorter.

## Online Ad Principles

### The One Purpose of an Ad

An ad exists to get the right people to click and the wrong people to keep scrolling. Targeting the right people lowers cost because fewer wrong people click. Curiosity is the single most reliable click driver.

### The Three-Step Ad Structure

Online ads need three moves, not the classic four-step AIDA model.

1. **Attention grabber** - use emotion. Talk about payoffs, penalties, outcomes, obstacles, wants, or fears. You cannot play it safe or try to be relevant to everyone. Force a decision.
2. **Curiosity driver** - make the reader ask "What is this? How do they do that?"
3. **Call to action** - one specific instruction: "Click here to..."

### Starting With a Question

If you do not know where to start, ask a question. The three core openers:

- Are you tired of [pain]?
- Would you like to [payoff]?
- Have you ever wanted to [result]?

A "yes" grabs attention and creates the opening for curiosity. A "no" costs you nothing because that person was never your buyer.

### Facebook and Instagram Ad Patterns

- The **image** is the real attention grabber on social feeds, not the headline. People stop for the picture first.
- In **video ads**, the first 3 seconds decide everything. That is why platforms measure success in 3-second views.
- Try **text-as-image**: plain black text on a white background with no photo. The text becomes the image and the hook at once.
- Lead with **emotion**: charged imagery, charged statements, and charged headlines. Avoid the safe middle.
- Pair a question headline with a curiosity payoff and a single click instruction.

### Ad Copy Patterns (Fill-in Templates)

- "Want the [number] [topic] secrets every [audience] needs?"
- "Free [resource] reveals how to [payoff] without [pain]. [CTA]."
- "[Number] ways to stop [painful activity]. [CTA]."
- "How to [result] without [obstacle]. [CTA]."
- "You can laugh at [problem] if you follow this simple plan. What is it? [CTA]."

### Ads Are a Numbers Game

- Expect to test **10 to 50 ads** to find one that converts profitably. Most people quit after two.
- Burn through losers fast, keep the winners, and scale the winners hard.
- Ads have a limited shelf life online. Always be testing new angles and emotional hooks.
- Think of ads like a gold-washing operation: run lots of dirt, sort, keep what pays, move on.

## Hooks and Angles

### What a Hook Is

A hook is a one-sentence story that creates intense curiosity. Its purpose is not to sell, convince, or convert. It only pulls people into the rest of your copy.

A hook is **not** a Unique Selling Proposition. The USP is what makes your product different from competitors. The hook is the lightning-fast story that makes people curious about that difference.

| Hook | USP |
| --- | --- |
| "One-legged golfer outdrives Tiger Woods." | "Our 3-minute video fixes 90% of long-drive problems." |
| "The story of a 76-year-old man who got me into the best shape of my life." | "A bodyweight exercise you will not see anywhere else." |

### Hook Formulas

Combine elements to build curiosity. Two reliable formulas:

### Unlikely character + timing + results

- "Former janitor goes from bankrupt to paid-off house in eighteen months with profits from his e-book business."
- "Ex-pizza delivery guy shows you 4 weird tricks to become a bestselling author in a weekend."

### Result + timeframe + pain removed

- "Lose all the weight you want in the next 30 days without diet or exercise."
- "Create and publish your outrageously profitable book in just three hours without writing a word."

### Hook Types

Use any of these to open an ad, email subject, headline, or social post.

- **Question** - ask the pain or desire question directly.
- **Warning** - alert the reader to a hidden risk or mistake.
- **Story** - one sentence about an unlikely character and a surprising result.
- **Statistic** - cite a number that reframes the problem.
- **Contrarian** - state the opposite of conventional wisdom.
- **How-to** - promise a specific method and timeframe.
- **Listicle** - lead with a numbered promise ("5 secrets every [audience] needs").
- **Testimonial** - open with a result someone else achieved.

### Where to Use a Hook

Anywhere you want to grab attention: headline, first paragraph, ad, email subject, social post, infographic, or video opener.

## Choosing the Right Angle

### The Ice Cream Principle

Your product is the ice cream. The angle is the flavor. You sell the same product to different audiences or in different formats by changing the angle, not the product.

- **Video sales letter** - fastest to build and test. Default first choice for anything under a couple hundred dollars.
- **Long-form sales letter** - use for higher-priced offers or when buyers need more information to decide.
- **Short-form sales letter** - use for inexpensive items or physical products that need little explanation.

You can run both a video and a long-form letter on the same page. Some people watch, some people read, some do both.

### Match the Angle to the Audience

- Identify the audience's dominant desire and dominant fear.
- Choose a hook type that speaks to that desire or fear.
- Pick the format that fits the price point and complexity.
- Test at least two angles against each other.

### Test, Do Not Assume

Nobody knows which angle will win until you test it. Beware of anyone who deals in absolutes about copy.

- Patterns and formulas are useful starting points.
- The only way to know what works for your audience is to test variants.
- Beware the trap of repeating one approach because it worked once. "Good enough" quietly kills results over time.
