# F.R.E.D. - Your Ideal Customer Avatar

> Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.

You sell to one person at a time. Millions may read your message, but the decision to buy happens in a single mind. Define that mind with precision, then write to it directly.

---

## The F.R.E.D. Acronym

F.R.E.D. is your avatar, your single ideal reader. The acronym maps the four inner territories you must occupy before you can sell anything:

- **F - Fears.** What keeps them up at night? What loss, failure, or pain are they trying to avoid? Fear is the stronger motivator; it outweighs desire roughly three to one. Name the specific fear, not the abstract one.
- **R - Real desires.** What do they *want* (not need)? People buy what they want and then justify the need. Sell the want. Fold the need into what you deliver.
- **E - End goals.** Where are they trying to go? What is the outcome on the far side of the gap they currently stand on? Describe the destination in their words.
- **D - Dreams.** What is the larger transformation, the identity or status shift, they secretly hope for? Dreams are the emotional payload beneath every end goal.

### Probing questions for each

**Fears:**

- What is the worst outcome they imagine if nothing changes?
- What have they already tried and failed at?
- What are they afraid others will think of them?
- What loss (money, time, status, relationship) do they fear most?

**Real desires:**

- What do they spend money on willingly in this area?
- What result do they tell their friends they want?
- What "magic pill" do they secretly wish existed?
- What would make them feel safe, secure, or in control?

**End goals:**

- What does success look like 30 days after buying?
- What metric or milestone tells them it worked?
- What will they stop doing once they have the result?
- What will they be free to start doing?

**Dreams:**

- Who do they want to *become*?
- What status or identity are they chasing?
- What story do they want to tell about themselves a year from now?

## PQRZ - The Operational Layer

F.R.E.D. tells you who they are. PQRZ tells you what is running through their head right now. Picture your reader on one cliff, the desired result on the opposite cliff. Your copy builds the bridge.

- **P - Problems.** The concrete pain points they live with daily.
- **Q - Questions.** The things they are actively searching for answers to.
- **R - Roadblocks.** The specific barriers holding them on the near cliff.
- **Z - Results.** The outcome on the far cliff they have not yet reached.

The reader is tuned exclusively to these four. Everything else is noise. Build your headlines, bullets, emails, and calls to action entirely from their PQRZ vocabulary.

### Where to find their PQRZ

- Read forums and answer sites (Quora, Yahoo Answers) for the exact questions they type.
- Mine your own help desk and support tickets for repeated complaints.
- Read one-star and five-star reviews of popular competing products.
- Run or borrow surveys; search `[keyword] survey` for existing data.
- Ask directly in social media groups and pages.
- Look at the table of contents and index of best-selling books in the niche.
- Pay attention at live events to what people complain about out loud.

For each of the four buckets, force yourself past the first two obvious answers. Aim for five to ten entries. The third, fourth, and fifth answers are where the real emotional connection hides.

## Avatar Questionnaire

Answer these to make F.R.E.D. whole before you write a single word of copy.

1. What is the micro-niche they belong to (narrower than the niche or sub-niche)?
2. Give the avatar a name. What do they call themselves (for example, "flipper," "couch potato," "green thumb")?
3. What are their top two to five problems right now?
4. What are their top two to five unanswered questions?
5. What are the top two to five roadblocks stopping them from getting results?
6. What are the top two to five results they desire but have not achieved?
7. What words and phrases do they use to describe their situation?
8. What do they fear losing most?
9. What do they want more than anything (the want, not the need)?
10. How do they spend their time and money in this area?
11. What have they already tried, and why did it fail them?
12. What would they stop doing the moment they had the result?
13. What transformation are they secretly hoping for?
14. What is the emotional shift they want (fear to security, stress to peace, pain to comfort, boredom to fun)?
15. Where do they hang out online and offline (to know how to reach them)?

## How to Use the Avatar in Copy

- **Mirror their language.** Use the exact words they use to describe their problems. When they hear their own vocabulary, they conclude you understand them and are not talking down to them.
- **Address specific objections.** Take each roadblock and knock it down explicitly in your copy. Show the answer to each question. Remove each barrier by name.
- **Build every element from their PQRZ.** Headlines, email subject lines, bullets, stories, and calls to action should all reference their problems, questions, roadblocks, and desired results. Nothing else earns their attention.
- **Sell the want, include the need.** Talk only about what they want. Deliver what they need inside the product. Never lecture them about what they need.
- **Shift the emotion.** Map the journey: from fear to security, stress to peace, pain to comfort, boredom to fun. Do not overlook fun; it is one of the most underused selling emotions.

## The "Write to One Person" Principle

You are always writing to a single reader, never to a crowd. Group-speak dilutes every sentence. Picture one specific person and write as if sending them a private message.

### The George technique

When you are stuck, write the copy as a message to one real or imaginary person who represents your audience. Open with a direct address to them by name. Keep it as casual as an email to a friend.

Example email structure that consistently outperforms:

- Subject: something genuinely useful you just found
- Body: "George, I just found this. I know you're into X. It does this, this, and this. Here's the link."
- Sign off as yourself.

Writing to George strips out the posturing, the jargon, and the bloat. The message tightens to only what that one person needs to hear. Emails written this way outperform polished, committee-style copy almost every time.

To make the avatar tangible, find a photo that represents them, print it out, and write to that face.

## Key Takeaways

- **A confused mind always says no.** Clarity beats cleverness every time.
- **Write at a grade 6 to 8 reading level.** Use short sentences, simple words, and sequential logic. You prove your intelligence by being understood, not by sounding smart.
- **Use their words, not yours.** Jargon, acronyms, and industry vocabulary make people feel stupid; people who feel stupid do not buy.
- **Make it all about them.** Hunt for "I, me, my, we, ours" in your draft and reframe to "you, your, yours." The reader is the hero of the message, not you.
- **Narrow is better.** A few feet wide and a mile deep beats a mile wide and three feet deep. Ten thousand laser-focused readers will always outsell one hundred thousand casually interested ones.
- **Test for clarity.** Hand the copy to someone outside your field. If they cannot follow it, rewrite it.
