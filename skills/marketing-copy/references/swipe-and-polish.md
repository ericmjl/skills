# Swipe File and Polish Reference

> Frameworks adapted from Jim Edwards' Copywriting Secrets.

## The Two Habits That Compound

Great copy comes from two repeatable habits: immersion in proven copy (your swipe file and research) and ruthless editing (the polish pass). Skip either and your copy lands flat. This reference covers how to build a swipe file, do immersive research, and run a final polish checklist.

---

## Swipe File Methodology

### What a Swipe File Is

A swipe file is a personal collection of ads, headlines, emails, sales pages, bullets, and any copy that grabbed your attention or is known to get results. It is not a library of clever quotes. It is a warmup tool. Reading good copy before you write primes the patterns your brain reaches for.

### What to Collect

- Headlines and subject lines
- Bullets (curiosity-inducing benefit statements)
- Full sales pages and video sales letter scripts
- Email subject lines and full email teasers
- Ad copy (social, search, and direct mail)
- Calls to action and guarantees
- Postscripts (P.S. blocks)
- Stories and opening hooks

### Where to Find Great Copy

- **Competitor funnels** - opt into their email lists, watch their ads, screenshot their sales pages.
- **Amazon** - bestseller back-cover copy, chapter titles, and reviews (see research below).
- **Ad libraries** - Facebook Ad Library, the Meta ad transparency tool, and competitor YouTube pre-roll.
- **Direct mail** - keep the postcards, catalogs, and sales letters that arrive in your mailbox.
- **Magazines and newsletters** - cover lines and bullets are headline labs.
- **Any medium, any industry** - your swipe file does not need to match your niche. A video-game magazine bullet can launch a six-figure headline in a different market.

### How to Organize a Swipe File

Use a digital folder with subfolders by copy type, plus a print folder for physical pieces.

- `headlines/`
- `subject-lines/`
- `bullets/`
- `ads/`
- `sales-pages/`
- `emails/`
- `calls-to-action/`
- `stories-hooks/`
- `my-stuff/` and `other-peoples-stuff/`

Capture on the go: photograph anything that stops you with your phone, email it to yourself, and file it the same day.

### How to Use a Swipe File

**Model, never copy.** Stealing words is plagiarism and it fails. The value is in the structure and psychology.

- Before writing headlines, read 10 headlines from your file.
- Before writing bullets, read 10 bullets.
- Before writing a sales page, read 2 or 3 full pages.
- Adapt the underlying pattern to your product, voice, and audience.

When a bullet says "The [topic] secrets you're not supposed to know," swap the topic and keep the curiosity structure. The frame is reusable; the words are yours to write.

## Immersive Research Techniques

### Why Research First

Never write copy cold. Research immerses you in the words, fears, desires, and objections of the people you are targeting. A few hours of research produces better copy, written faster. Skipping it means your copy will not resonate and people will not buy.

### The Three Questions to Answer

Before writing, find out:

- What do they **want** more than anything?
- What do they **fear**?
- What are their **objections** to buying what you (or anyone) sells?

### Amazon Review Mining

Amazon is the single richest research source because it combines bestseller rankings with verbatim customer feedback.

1. Find the top-selling products in your niche.
2. Read the back-cover copy and chapter titles to see what hooks already convert.
3. Read **5-star reviews** to learn what excites buyers and what words they use.
4. Read **1-star reviews** to learn what angers people and what to avoid or counter.
5. Read **2-, 3-, and 4-star reviews** for the gold: people who liked parts and disliked parts. They explain gaps you can exploit.

Use the customers' exact words in your copy. If reviewers complain a competitor's course sounds like "straight out of a classroom," your headline becomes "How to learn conversational [topic] in as little as two weeks."

### Competitor Funnel Analysis

This process is called funnel hacking.

- Opt into competitor email lists and save every email.
- Click their ads and screenshot the landing pages.
- Buy or review their product to experience the full funnel.
- Note their hooks, bullets, offers, guarantees, and upsells.
- Model their approach. Do not steal their words.

Look for gaps: if customers complain about a competitor, a single line in your copy ("Unlike other training, we don't try to sell you anything extra") can win the sale.

### Forum and Social Media Listening

- Search Google for blog posts, ads, and keywords around the problem.
- Read the questions people ask in forums, groups, and comment threads.
- Mine your own support tickets, FAQs, and customer emails. These are a goldmine of objections and real language.
- Pay attention to negative feedback on your own products. It reveals copy gaps competitors could exploit.

### Make Research Part of the Process

Dedicate a block of time before every copy project. If you are writing a sales letter, research popular sales letters. If you are writing an email, research popular emails. The warmup is the work.

## Polish Checklist

### Why Polish Matters

People judge your product by the quality of your words. Typos, grammar errors, and broken formatting signal a low-quality product. Polishing is not optional; it is how you protect the sale you already earned with your message.

### The Editing Pass

Run this checklist on your completed first draft. Finish the draft first, then edit.

### Voice and verbs

- [ ] Replace passive voice with active voice.
- [ ] Strengthen weak verbs. Swap "is," "was," "has," and "make" for specific action verbs.
- [ ] Cut filler words: "very," "really," "that," "just," "actually," "quite."

### Length and rhythm

- [ ] Tighten long sentences into short ones.
- [ ] Break long paragraphs into one- to three-sentence blocks.
- [ ] Vary sentence length so the rhythm does not drone.
- [ ] Read the copy aloud. Fix anything that trips your tongue.

### Clarity and specificity

- [ ] Make every claim specific. Replace vague statements with numbers, names, and concrete details.
- [ ] Add transition words so each section flows into the next.
- [ ] Aim for a grade 6 to 8 reading level on a readability checker. Complex ideas in simple sentences beat simple ideas in complex sentences.

### Scannability (the secondary reading path)

- [ ] Read only the headline, sub-headlines, bolded words, and the P.S. Can a skimmer still grasp the offer? If not, rewrite.
- [ ] Check that bullets and sub-headlines each carry weight on their own.

### Proofreading and format

- [ ] Run a grammar and spell check.
- [ ] Check punctuation and formatting.
- [ ] View the copy in multiple browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari) and on multiple devices (phone, tablet, desktop).
- [ ] Get a second pair of eyes on the copy. Another reader catches errors you are blind to.

### Video and mobile

- [ ] If you have a video sales letter, watch it with the sound off. Add subtitles so the message still lands.
- [ ] Check how the copy looks on a phone screen. Most readers are on mobile.

### The Slippery Slide Test

Read the finished copy start to finish. Does the reader slide from the headline to the buy button without friction? Fix anything that causes a visual, grammatical, or tonal snag. The copy should feel like a conversation with no jarring breaks between sections.

### The Litmus Test

If a target customer reads the copy and does not ask "Where can I get that?", the copy is not done. Go back to the research, strengthen the hook, and run the polish pass again.
