# Counterpart styles

Identifying the counterpart's default style changes how you deploy every technique. There are three types, and most people have a primary with a secondary.

## The Analyst

**Signals:** Methodical, data-heavy communication. Asks detailed questions. Hates surprises. Takes time to respond. May seem cold or distant. Prefers email over phone. Sends long, carefully worded messages.

**Their fear:** Being rushed into errors, looking unprepared, making a decision without sufficient data.

**Their view of time:** "As long as it takes to get it right."

**How to adapt:**
- Lead with data and specifics. They process information, not emotions (at least overtly).
- Give them processing time. Don't interpret silence as anger — they're thinking.
- Be patient. Pushing them triggers withdrawal, not action.
- Avoid emotional appeals — they see these as manipulation.
- Provide written follow-ups after verbal conversations so they can analyze at their pace.
- Labels work with Analysts but should reference observable patterns, not raw emotions. "It seems like the timeline data is raising some concerns" lands better than "It seems like you're frustrated."

**Common pitfall when YOU are the Analyst:**
- You over-prepare and under-connect. The counterpart may feel you're cold or that you don't care about the relationship.
- You avoid confrontation by retreating into data, which can read as passive aggression.

---

## The Assertive

**Signals:** Direct, fast-paced, time-conscious. Wants to get to the point. May interrupt. Frames things as winning or losing. Short emails. Impatient with small talk. Values their own time above most things.

**Their fear:** Wasting time, being seen as weak, losing control of the situation.

**Their view of time:** "Time is money. Get this done now."

**How to adapt:**
- Be concise. Get to the point quickly.
- Acknowledge their points first — they will not listen to you until they feel heard. This is the single most important adaptation with Assertives.
- Use mirrors and labels — they respond well to being reflected.
- Stand your ground without being combative. They actually respect firmness more than accommodation.
- Match their pace in conversation, but use silence strategically — the contrast will land harder.
- Calibrated questions are powerful with Assertives because these questions give them the feeling of being in charge while directing the conversation.

**Common pitfall when YOU are the Assertive:**
- You're so focused on being heard that you miss the counterpart's actual position.
- You mistake accommodation for weakness and push too hard, damaging the relationship.
- You dismiss the Analyst's need for time and the Accommodator's need for rapport.

---

## The Accommodator

**Signals:** Friendly, talkative, relationship-focused. Quick to agree. Avoids confrontation. Lots of "sounds great" and "absolutely." Values the relationship above the deal. Wants to be liked.

**Their fear:** Damaging the relationship, confrontation, being seen as difficult.

**Their view of time:** "As long as we're connecting, it's time well spent."

**How to adapt:**
- Build genuine rapport first. They need to feel the relationship is solid before doing business.
- Verify every agreement. Their quick "yes" is often counterfeit — they say yes to maintain harmony, not because they'll follow through.
- Pin down specifics and implementation details. Apply the Rule of Three.
- Be warm but direct. They appreciate kindness but need you to be clear about expectations.
- Non-monetary gestures and relationship signals matter enormously to Accommodators.
- If you need to push back, do it within the context of the relationship: "Because this relationship matters to me, I want to make sure we get this right" before the difficult point.

**Common pitfall when YOU are the Accommodator:**
- You give away too much to keep the peace.
- Your "yes" sometimes isn't real — you agree in the moment to avoid discomfort and then can't deliver.
- You avoid necessary difficult conversations until the problem is much bigger than it needed to be.

## Identifying your own style

Help the user self-assess by asking:

1. When under pressure in a conversation, do you tend to: (a) withdraw and analyze, (b) push harder and get direct, or (c) smooth things over and defer?
2. What frustrates you most about other people in conversations: (a) when they're unprepared or imprecise, (b) when they waste your time, or (c) when they're cold or adversarial?
3. In email, do you tend to: (a) write long, detailed messages, (b) write short, action-oriented messages, or (c) include personal check-ins and warmth?

Mostly (a) = Analyst. Mostly (b) = Assertive. Mostly (c) = Accommodator.

**Key principle:** Your natural style is not the default communication mode for other humans. Adapt to theirs. The Analyst who communicates with data is perceived as cold by the Accommodator. The Assertive who pushes for speed is perceived as reckless by the Analyst. The Accommodator who builds rapport is perceived as wasting time by the Assertive.

The goal is not to change your type but to recognize what others need and flex toward it.

## Style interaction dynamics

| You → Them | Key adaptation |
|---|---|
| Analyst → Assertive | Be more concise. Lead with the bottom line, then offer data as backup. |
| Analyst → Accommodator | Add personal warmth. Start with connection, then transition to data. |
| Assertive → Analyst | Slow down. Provide data before pushing for decisions. |
| Assertive → Accommodator | Soften your tone. Build rapport before making demands. |
| Accommodator → Analyst | Get specific. Pin down details instead of assuming agreement. |
| Accommodator → Assertive | Be more direct. They respect clarity over warmth. |
