# Channel-adaptive negotiation

The 7-38-55 rule (Albert Mehrabian) says communication is 7% words, 38% tone of voice, and 55% body language. Different channels strip away different layers, so your technique deployment must adapt.

## In person

**You have:** Words + Tone + Body language = 100% communication bandwidth

**Technique strengths:**
- Mirroring is at its most powerful (curious tone + silence creates irresistible vacuum)
- Dynamic silence has maximum impact — 4+ seconds of silence in person is almost unbearable to the counterpart; they will fill it
- Body language incongruence detection is available — watch for mismatches between words and physical signals
- The late-night DJ voice has its full involuntary calming effect

**Key adaptations:**
- This is your highest-leverage channel. Use it for the most important conversations.
- Watch their body language for Black Swans — crossed arms, avoiding eye contact, tension in face/shoulders
- Mirror their body posture subtly (this is body-language mirroring, different from verbal mirroring) to build subconscious rapport
- Position yourself at an angle rather than directly facing them — direct face-to-face can feel adversarial

**When to recommend in-person:** High-stakes negotiations, relationship repair, situations with many unknowns where you need maximum information bandwidth.

## Video call

**You have:** Words + Tone + Partial body language (~65% bandwidth)

**What's lost:**
- Lower body language (can't see fidgeting, posture shifts below chest)
- Spatial proximity cues
- Micro-expressions are harder to read through compression artifacts
- "Side conversations" and hallway moments don't exist

**Technique strengths:**
- Labeling still works well — you can observe facial reactions
- Calibrated questions are effective — you get tonal and facial feedback
- Accusation audit has good impact since they can see your sincerity

**Key adaptations:**
- Camera position matters. Eye-level or slightly above. Looking down at a laptop screen reads as disengaged.
- Silence is slightly less powerful than in-person because the counterpart has escape routes (muting, looking at other screens)
- Pay extra attention to eye movement — where do they look when answering tough questions?
- The chat sidebar can be used strategically — sending a key number or question in writing while discussing verbally creates a dual-channel anchor

**When to recommend video:** Good default for remote negotiations. Better than phone when you need to read reactions.

## Phone call

**You have:** Words + Tone only (~45% bandwidth)

**What's lost:**
- All body language
- Visual incongruence detection
- The counterpart's environment and physical state

**Technique strengths:**
- The late-night DJ voice is disproportionately powerful on the phone — without visual distraction, tone becomes the dominant channel
- Mirroring works excellently — the verbal nature of mirroring is a perfect fit
- Dynamic silence is very effective on phone — the counterpart cannot see you thinking, so silence feels more significant
- Labels are effective because tone of voice carries 38% of their response

**Key adaptations:**
- Your voice IS your body language. Vary pace, volume, and tone deliberately.
- Smile while speaking — it changes your vocal quality even though they can't see you
- Standing while talking gives you more vocal energy than sitting
- Take notes on their exact words — you'll need them for mirrors and labels

**When to recommend phone:** When you want the power of voice without the performance of video. Good for difficult conversations where you want the counterpart focused on your words and tone, not your face.

## Email

**You have:** Words only (~7% of original bandwidth)

**What's lost:**
- All tone (words carry the entire weight)
- All body language
- Real-time feedback loop
- Ability to course-correct in the moment

**This is the hardest channel to negotiate through.** Every word matters far more than in any other medium.

**Technique deployment rules for email:**

1. **Labeling:** Works well. Use as paragraph openers. "It seems like the timeline is creating pressure for your team." In email, a label slows the reader down and makes them feel seen.

2. **Calibrated questions:** Must be the LAST line. A calibrated question buried in paragraph two gets skipped. One question per email. End the email after the question — this is the written equivalent of silence.

3. **Accusation audit:** A one-line mini audit in the opening is effective. "I know this probably seems like I'm just looking out for myself here."

4. **No-oriented questions:** Work well in subject lines and closing lines. As a re-engagement tactic in email, they're extremely effective.

5. **Mirroring:** Least effective in email. Without tone, mirroring reads as confused or sarcastic. Avoid.

6. **Dynamic silence:** Translates to ending the email after your key point. No softening paragraph, no "let me know if you have questions," no pleasantries after the main move. The email ends where the silence would be.

**Email-specific rules:**
- Less is more. Short emails outperform long ones. Every sentence must earn its place.
- One technique per email, maximum. The "kitchen sink" email that deploys labeling + accusation audit + calibrated question reads as a negotiation textbook, not a person.
- The last line is the most important line. What you write last is what they think about.
- Match the user's natural writing style. If they write casually, draft casually. The techniques must be invisible.
- Set it up before you land it. Build context in the first line or two before your tactical move.
- End with encouragement or a positive note when possible (unless the strategic intent requires tension).

## Text message / chat (Slack, iMessage, WhatsApp, etc.)

**You have:** Words only, with expectation of brevity and speed

**Even more constrained than email.** The norms of texting expect short messages, quick responses, and casual tone.

**What works:**
- No-oriented questions are ideal for text. "Is it a bad time to bring this up?" or "Have you given up on [thing]?" fit the medium perfectly.
- Labels work in one-line form. "Sounds like that was frustrating."
- Brief accusation audits. "I know this is annoying to bring up again but..."

**What doesn't work:**
- Anything longer than 2-3 sentences. You lose them.
- Calibrated questions feel heavy in text. Simplify them.
- Multiple techniques in one message. One move. That's it.

**Key adaptation:**
- Text exchanges unfold over multiple messages. Each message is one micro-move. Think of it as a chess game where each text is one move, not a board position.

## Asynchronous contexts (letters, proposals, contracts)

These are one-shot communications where you don't get real-time feedback.

**What works:**
- Accusation audits in cover letters or proposal intros.
- Fairness framing early in the document.
- Loss aversion framing in the body — what they stand to lose by not acting.
- Precise numbers (Ackerman-style) in any monetary proposal.
- The entire document should be structured to build toward a "That's right" moment — by the time they finish reading, they should feel you understand their situation deeply.

**What doesn't work:**
- Mirroring (no conversation to mirror)
- Dynamic silence (they control the pacing)
- Calibrated questions (no real-time answer — though one strategic question at the end of a proposal can prompt a call)

## Channel selection strategy

When advising the user, consider which channel gives them the best advantage:

| Situation | Recommended channel | Why |
|---|---|---|
| High stakes, many unknowns | In person | Maximum bandwidth for discovery and influence |
| Emotional or relationship-sensitive | Phone or in person | Tone carries empathy better than text |
| Need a paper trail | Email | Documentation, plus time to craft responses |
| Re-engaging someone who's gone silent | Email or text | No-oriented question technique works perfectly in writing |
| Price negotiation | Phone or in person | Ackerman model needs real-time back-and-forth |
| Delivering bad news | Phone (then follow up in writing) | Tone softens the blow; email documents the details |
| Initial outreach | Email with one calibrated question | Low pressure, gives them time to respond |
| Ongoing relationship management | Mix of channels | Variety signals genuine interest, not just transactional contact |
