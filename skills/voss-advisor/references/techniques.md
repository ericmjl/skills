## 1. Tactical empathy

**What:** Demonstrating that you understand the counterpart's feelings and perspective. Not agreement. Not sympathy. Demonstrated comprehension of their world.

**When:** Always. This is the foundation, not an occasional tactic. Especially critical when the counterpart is emotional, defensive, or entrenched.

**The structural move:**
1. Observe their emotional state from words, tone, behavior, context
2. Articulate what they are likely feeling and why
3. Demonstrate this understanding
4. Allow space for them to confirm, correct, or expand

**Deployment conditions:**
- Must be genuine. Performed empathy is detected and backfires.
- Must focus on THEIR world, not yours. The moment you pivot to your needs, empathy ends.
- In writing: open with their perspective before introducing yours.

**What NOT to do:**
- "I understand how you feel." — Cliche that signals the opposite of understanding.
- "I hear you, but..." — "But" negates everything before it.
- Using empathy as a manipulation preamble — demonstrating understanding only to immediately pivot to your ask makes them feel ambushed, not understood.

## 2. Mirroring

**What:** Repeating the last 1-3 critical words of what the counterpart said, with curious, slightly upward inflection. This is verbal repetition, not body language mirroring.

**When:**
- When you need them to elaborate without asking a direct question
- When you need time to think
- When a statement contains something surprising or important
- In early conversation stages for rapport building

**The structural move:**
1. Listen for the key phrase or emotionally loaded words
2. Repeat those 1-3 words as a gentle question
3. Go silent for at least 4 seconds
4. Let them fill the space

**The four-step disagreement process (disagree without being disagreeable):**
1. Use the late-night DJ voice (warm, slow, downward inflection)
2. Begin with "I'm sorry..."
3. Mirror their statement
4. Silence (minimum four seconds)

**Deployment conditions:**
- Tone must be curious, not challenging.
- Works best with warm, inquisitive delivery.
- In writing: less effective. Use labeling or calibrated questions instead.

**What NOT to do:**
- Mirroring too many words — repeating a full sentence sounds like mockery. Stick to 1-3 words.
- Mirroring with flat or aggressive tone — turns rapport into interrogation.
- Mirroring in email — without vocal tone, it reads as confused or sarcastic.

## 3. Labeling

**What:** Naming the counterpart's emotion or perspective using tentative, third-person phrasing.

**Starter phrases:** "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", "It looks like...", "It feels like..."

**Why third-person:** Starting with "I" ("What I'm hearing is...") centers on you and triggers defensiveness.

**When:**
- When you detect an emotion driving their position
- To deactivate negative emotions (fear, frustration, suspicion)
- To amplify positive emotions (excitement, relief, pride)
- When you want to prompt information release without asking a direct question

**The structural move:**
1. Identify the emotion or unstated concern
2. Name it tentatively using the starter phrases
3. Go silent. Let the label work. Minimum 4 seconds in conversation.
4. Listen — confirmation, correction, or elaboration are all useful.

**Advanced move — Intentional mislabel:** People correct mislabels more openly than they answer direct questions. Deliberately (gently) mischaracterizing their position can unlock their real stance.

**Deployment conditions:**
- Always tentative, never declarative.
- In email: labels work well as paragraph openers. They slow the reader down and make them feel seen.
- After a label, never follow with "but." Silence or a new paragraph.

**What NOT to do:**
- "I think you're feeling..." — Centers on your perception, not their experience.
- Labeling then immediately pivoting to your ask — weaponizes empathy.
- Over-labeling — three labels in a row without space for response sounds scripted.

## 4. Calibrated questions

**What:** Open-ended questions starting with "How" or "What" that give the counterpart the illusion of control while directing the conversation.

**Words to avoid starting with:** "Can," "Is," "Are," "Do," "Does" (invite yes/no). "Why" (triggers defensiveness — converts to accusation in the listener's brain).

**When:**
- To push back without saying no
- To uncover hidden constraints or decision-making structures
- To make the counterpart solve your problem for you
- To verify commitment
- To buy time

**Essential patterns by purpose:**

| Purpose | Question structure |
|---|---|
| Push back on demands | Ask how you're supposed to meet that constraint |
| Uncover needs | Ask what about this matters most to them |
| Open a negotiation | Ask what their biggest challenge is |
| Create collaboration | Ask how you can solve the problem together |
| Verify commitment | Ask how you'll both know things are on track |
| Surface decision-makers | Ask how this affects the rest of their team |
| Explore implementation | Ask what happens if this doesn't get done |

**"Why" exception:** Use "why" only when it benefits you for them to defend a position favorable to you. Making them articulate your value.

**Deployment conditions:**
- In conversation: one calibrated question, then silence. Never stack.
- In email: the calibrated question must be the LAST line. Buried mid-paragraph, it gets ignored. One question per email.
- Requires genuine curiosity. If you already know the answer, use a label instead.

**What NOT to do:**
- Stacking questions — they answer the easiest and ignore the rest.
- "Why did you...?" — Sounds accusatory. Convert to "What caused...?" or "What led to...?"
- Using calibrated questions when a direct statement is needed — sometimes clarity requires a clear position.

## 5. Accusation audit

**What:** Proactively listing every negative thing the counterpart might think about you or your position before they say it.

**When:**
- Opening of difficult conversations
- Before making an ask that could be perceived negatively
- Re-engaging after a conflict or silence
- In emails where the request could seem self-serving

**The structural move:**
1. Before the interaction, brainstorm: what is the worst they could think about me?
2. List these negatives explicitly and early
3. Use observation language ("It probably seems like...", "You might think...")
4. Go silent after delivering. Do NOT follow with "but." Do not defend.

**Deployment conditions:**
- The accusations should sound slightly exaggerated. This encourages the counterpart to correct toward the positive.
- In email: a one-line mini accusation audit in the opening works well.
- Never use denial framing.

**What NOT to do:**
- "I don't want you to feel like..." — Denial framing plants the exact thought. Use observation framing: "It might seem like..."
- Immediately defending after the audit — needs silence.
- Half-hearted audit that skips the real fears — the big unspoken one festers.

## 6. No-oriented questions

**What:** Questions designed so the comfortable, safe answer is "no" — but "no" advances your position.

**When:**
- To open conversations
- To re-engage silent counterparts
- To make proposals feel low-pressure
- When the counterpart seems defensive or cornered

**The structural move:** Invert the framing so "no" = engagement.
- Instead of "Would you agree to X?" → "Would it be unreasonable to X?"
- Instead of "Do you have a minute?" → "Is now a bad time?"

**Re-engagement pattern:** When someone has gone silent, a no-oriented question that implies they've quit forces a corrective "no." Example structure: ask if they've given up on the project/deal/conversation.

**The three types of "yes":**

| Type | What it means | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Counterfeit | Escaping the conversation | No follow-through |
| Confirmation | Reflexive agreement | No commitment |
| Commitment | Genuine intent to act | The only one that matters |

**Deployment conditions:**
- In email: works well as subject lines or closing lines.
- The question must be genuinely answerable with "no."
- "Have you given up on...?" requires existing relationship context.

## 7. "That's right" breakthrough

**What:** Getting the counterpart to say "That's right" by summarizing their position, emotions, and worldview so accurately they feel completely understood.

**Critical distinction:**
- "That's right" = breakthrough. Trust established. Deadlocks break.
- "You're right" = dismissal. They want you to stop talking. Nothing changes.

**The structural move:**
1. Listen deeply throughout the interaction
2. Paraphrase their position including emotional subtext
3. Deliver as a summary, not a question
4. Refine until you hear "that's right"

**In writing:** A summary paragraph demonstrating full comprehension of their position, delivered before you introduce yours.

## 8. Ackerman model (monetary negotiation)

**The sequence:**
1. Set your target price (your real goal)
2. First offer at 65% of target
3. Three calculated raises: 85%, 95%, 100%
4. Use empathy techniques and varied "no" framings before each raise — make them counter before you move
5. Final number must be precise and non-round (e.g., $37,263 not $37,000)
6. Final offer includes a non-monetary sweetener

**Why it works:**
- Decreasing increments (20% then 10% then 5%) create the impression you're being squeezed to your absolute limit
- Precise numbers feel researched and final, not arbitrary
- The non-monetary sweetener signals you've truly exhausted your financial range

**Deployment conditions:**
- Only for monetary negotiations with back-and-forth offers
- Requires patience — absorb their counter-offers between moves
- In email: each move should be a separate exchange, not laid out in one message

## 9. Bending reality (loss aversion)

**What:** Using the psychological asymmetry between gains and losses to shift the counterpart's perception. People will take more risks to avoid a loss than to achieve a gain of equal magnitude.

**The framework:**
1. Start with a foundation of empathy — this is not a manipulation trick, it's a framing tool
2. Understand what the counterpart stands to lose (not just what they want to gain)
3. Frame your proposal in terms of what they avoid losing, not what they gain
4. Use anchoring to shift their perceived range of possible outcomes

**How loss aversion manifests:**
- Fear of loss keeps people up at night in a way hope of gain does not
- People make irrational decisions to avoid perceived losses
- The pain of losing $100 is psychologically about twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining $100

**Activation patterns:**
- "If we don't move forward, you'd lose [specific thing they value]..."
- Deadlines that are real (not artificial) create genuine loss aversion
- Showing what comparable counterparts have achieved (normative leverage) makes inaction feel like falling behind

**Extreme anchoring:**
- A deliberately extreme initial offer "bends reality" by shifting the counterpart's perceived range
- Must be combined with tactical empathy so it doesn't feel aggressive
- The anchor should be defensible with some logic, even if extreme

**When to deploy:**
- When the counterpart is comfortable with the status quo and has no urgency to act
- When you need to shift the frame from "what do I gain" to "what do I lose"
- When logical arguments aren't moving them (because logic isn't driving their decision)

## 10. Dynamic silence

**What:** Deliberate, extended pauses after key statements, labels, mirrors, or questions. Minimum 4 seconds in conversation.

**Why it works:** Silence creates a vacuum. People fill vacuums with information, concessions, or their internal reasoning made external.

**When to deploy:**
- After every mirror
- After every label
- After every calibrated question
- After an accusation audit (never "but")
- The urge to fill silence is your enemy. Resist it.

**In writing:** Silence translates to ending the email after your key question or label. No softening paragraph after your main point. Let the message end where the silence would be.

## 11. Black Swan discovery

**What:** Hidden information that, if discovered, would fundamentally change the negotiation. Every negotiation has approximately three.

**How to find them:**
- Stay in discovery mode longer than feels comfortable
- Listen for anomalies — unexpected reactions, dodged topics, offhand comments
- Pay attention to unguarded moments (before/after formal meetings, hallway conversation)
- When behavior seems irrational, assume it is not — search for: hidden constraints, hidden desires, or bad information they're operating on

**Three types of leverage Black Swans create:**
- Positive: you can give them what they secretly need
- Negative: you can threaten what they fear losing (use with extreme caution)
- Normative: their actions contradict their stated values (most elegant)

## 12. Rule of Three

**What:** Getting agreement to the same thing three times in one conversation, using three different framings.

1. Direct agreement to the proposal
2. A summary that triggers "That's right"
3. A calibrated question about implementation

Genuine commitments survive all three. If agreement collapses on round two or three, you never had a real deal.

## 13. Fairness framing

**What:** Proactively establishing fairness norms before "fair" becomes a weapon.

**Recommended framing (deliver early):** Communicate that your goal is for them to feel treated fairly throughout, and that if at any point they don't, they should tell you so you can address it.

**Three adversarial uses of "fair" and responses:**

| Their move | Meaning | Response structure |
|---|---|---|
| "We just want what's fair" | Guilt weapon — implying you've been unfair | Pause. Ask where specifically you treated them unfairly. Offer to revisit. |
| "This is a fair offer" | Anchoring — discouraging further negotiation | Mirror "fair?" with silence. Ask how they arrived at that assessment. |
| "That's not fair" | Emotional reaction — violated norms | Stop. Acknowledge. Explore what triggered the perception. |
